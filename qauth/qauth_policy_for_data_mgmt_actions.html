      • Quorum authorization policy

        A quorum authorization policy is the means by which quorum authorization is enforced. To ensure that a single user does not run data-modifying actions, a quorum authorization policy specifies such actions that require additional approval.

        • Quorum authorization management actions

          Actions related to the management of quorum authorization policies and roles require additional approvals. For such actions, RSC enforces the requirement of additional approvals through policies. Only a user with the QAuth administrator role can approve or deny requests to perform these actions.

        • System configuration quorum authorization policies

          A quorum authorization policy for system configuration actions is a user-defined policy that prevents a user from running actions related to system configuration without the required approvals. The policy applies to actions related to system configuration for the connected Rubrik clusters and to global configuration actions for RSC.

        • Data management quorum authorization policies

          A quorum authorization policy for data management actions is a user-defined policy that prevents a user from running data management actions on specific objects without the required approvals.

        • Quorum authorization and multitenancy

          In a multitenant environment, when quorum authorization is enabled in the global organization, the quorum authorization policies also apply to the actions initiated in the tenant organizations.

      • Quorum authorization roles

        Quorum authorization roles in RSC provide structured management of approvals and permissions, ensuring appropriate reviews for system changes. The QAuth administrator role oversees quorum authorization activities. The QAuth approver role, which can be added to custom roles, allows users to approve specific policy changes.

      • Quorum authorization process

        The Quorum Authorization (QAuth) process secures actions related to data management and system configuration. This involves enabling QAuth, creating and associating QAuth policies and roles, requesting and reviewing actions, and optionally excluding service accounts to ensure automation scripts are not blocked.

      • Initial setup of quorum authorization

        The initial setup of Quorum Authorization (QAuth) involves identifying and configuring primary and backup QAuth administrators, ensuring they do not have conflicting privileges, and enabling the QAuth feature to manage secure user authentication and administrative requests.

      • Configuration of quorum authorization policies and roles

        Configuring quorum authorization policies and roles enforces comprehensive data governance, enabling RSC administrators to centrally control, customize, and secure data management and system configuration actions.

      • Quorum authorization requests and approvals

        When a requester attempts an action protected by a policy, RSC triggers a quorum authorization request. The requester cannot perform the action until the request is approved by a user with the QAuth approver role.

      • Retention lock and quorum authorization

        The retention lock feature helps organizations meet legal and compliance standards by providing Compliance and Governance modes, both requiring quorum authorization approvals for specific actions.

Data management quorum authorization policies

A quorum authorization policy for data management actions is a user-defined policy that prevents a user from running data management actions on specific objects without the required approvals.

Quorum Authorization (QAuth) policies are essential for protecting data management actions on objects. As a user with the global RSC administrator role, you can create these policies to ensure that certain critical actions require additional approval before proceeding. When creating the policy, you can select specific objects directly, or you can select objects indirectly by selecting Rubrik clusters. When you select a Rubrik cluster, the policy applies to all the objects protected by the selected cluster. The QAuth policies apply to all objects in the object hierarchy.

QAuth policy for Objects

QAuth policies for Objects protect actions directly on objects or their parent entities, such as a vSphere virtual machine and its vCenter. These policies cover actions such as deleting a snapshot or assigning an SLA Domain. They do not, however, protect against changes to the definition of an SLA Domain, such as editing its retention or frequency.

For complete protection, you must create a separate QAuth policy for the SLA Domain itself. This ensures any edits to the SLA Domain settings trigger an approval request. You can apply an object policy directly to specific objects, or indirectly by including a parent entity such as a vCenter, which covers all descendant objects.

Action Description
Delete Snapshot Applies to the snapshots of the selected objects. The descendants of the selected objects inherit the policy from the parents.
Change legal hold status Applies to the snapshots of selected objects. The descendants of the selected objects inherit the policy from the parents.
Assign SLA Domain Applies to objects. If you are creating the policy for specific objects, you can select only those objects that have a direct SLA Domain assignment. The QAuth policy rules then apply to those objects and all of their descendants.

Assigning an SLA Domain to the selected objects or to the descendants of the objects requires QAuth.
Make restricted changes to objects or snapshots assigned a retention-locked SLA Domain Applies to objects. If you are creating the policy for specific objects, you can select only those objects that have a direct SLA Domain assignment. The QAuth policy rules then apply to those objects and all of their descendants.

Changing or removing the retention lock for the selected objects or their descendants requires QAuth.

QAuth policy for Clusters

QAuth policies for Clusters ensure protection for actions on objects within selected clusters, serving as optimization for uniform policies across all current and future objects. They operate similarly to Objects policies, with broader scope efficiency.

Action Description
Assign SLA Domain Applies to objects in the selected clusters.

Assigning an SLA Domain to the selected clusters requires QAuth.
Make restricted changes to objects or snapshots assigned a retention-locked SLA Domain Applies to objects in the selected clusters.

Changing or removing the retention lock requires QAuth. All objects within the clusters inherit the policy.
Delete Snapshot Applies to the snapshots of the objects in the selected clusters. All objects within the clusters inherit the policy.
Change legal hold status Applies to the snapshots of objects in the selected clusters. All objects within the clusters inherit the policy.

QAuth policy for SLA Domains

QAuth policies for SLA Domains protect actions related directly to the SLA Domain definition. These policies do not cover actions taken directly on the objects assigned to the SLA Domain. For example, these policies do not protect the deletion of a snapshot since it is not an action taken directly on the SLA Domain. They do protect against an SLA Domain edit, such as reducing retention, from causing snapshots to expire.

Action Description
Make restricted changes to retention-locked SLA Domain Applies to selected SLA Domains.
Edit SLA Domain Applies to selected SLA Domains.
Pause SLA Domain protection Applies to selected SLA Domains.

Recommendations and best practices

For complete object-level security, Rubrik recommends creating QAuth policies for an object and for the SLA Domains assigned to the object. For example, an object-level QAuth policy that requires additional approval before deleting the snapshots of an object will not be triggered when snapshots are deleted as a result of changing the snapshot retention period in an SLA Domain that is assigned to that object. For this scenario, you require one QAuth policy at the object level, which requires additional approval to delete a snapshot, and another policy at the SLA Domain level, which requires additional approval to edit the SLA Domain assigned to the object.

Some key best practices include:
  • Creating combined policies: Always creating a combination of SLA Domains and Objects or Clusters policies for comprehensive object-level protection.
  • Starting small: Beginning with policies that have a small scope, such as a single SLA Domain or small sets of virtual machines, to better understand the workflow and request approval processes.

Policies visibility and approval

To view the list of QAuth policies in RSC, a user account must have a role with the permission to view QAuth policies. To approve or deny QAuth requests triggered by certain policies, a user account must have the QAuth approver role created for those policies.

Related tasks
  • Creating a quorum authorization policy for data management actions
    To prevent specific data management actions from being performed by a single user, create a policy for quorum authorization of such actions. The policy includes the actions that you want to protect, the objects whose data you want to protect, and the service accounts that do not require quorum authorization for performing the selected actions on the selected objects.
  • Pausing SLA Domain protection
    To temporarily stop protection for specific objects in a Rubrik cluster, pause the protection activity of the SLA Domain that protects those objects. This action only pauses the protection activity for the objects under that SLA Domain, while other objects in the cluster protected by different SLA Domains remain unaffected.
  • Removing a legal hold from a snapshot
    Remove a legal hold from a snapshot to return to normal retention management for that snapshot.
