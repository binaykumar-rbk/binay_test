      • Quorum authorization policy

        A quorum authorization policy is the means by which quorum authorization is enforced. To ensure that a single user does not run data-modifying actions, a quorum authorization policy specifies such actions that require additional approval.

      • Quorum authorization roles

        Quorum authorization roles in RSC provide structured management of approvals and permissions, ensuring appropriate reviews for system changes. The QAuth administrator role oversees quorum authorization activities. The QAuth approver role, which can be added to custom roles, allows users to approve specific policy changes.

      • Quorum authorization process

        The Quorum Authorization (QAuth) process secures actions related to data management and system configuration. This involves enabling QAuth, creating and associating QAuth policies and roles, requesting and reviewing actions, and optionally excluding service accounts to ensure automation scripts are not blocked.

      • Initial setup of quorum authorization

        The initial setup of Quorum Authorization (QAuth) involves identifying and configuring primary and backup QAuth administrators, ensuring they do not have conflicting privileges, and enabling the QAuth feature to manage secure user authentication and administrative requests.

      • Configuration of quorum authorization policies and roles

        Configuring quorum authorization policies and roles enforces comprehensive data governance, enabling RSC administrators to centrally control, customize, and secure data management and system configuration actions.

      • Quorum authorization requests and approvals

        When a requester attempts an action protected by a policy, RSC triggers a quorum authorization request. The requester cannot perform the action until the request is approved by a user with the QAuth approver role.

      • Retention lock and quorum authorization

        The retention lock feature helps organizations meet legal and compliance standards by providing Compliance and Governance modes, both requiring quorum authorization approvals for specific actions.

Key actors and responsibilities in quorum authorization

Understanding the roles and responsibilities of key actors in quorum authorization management is important to ensure a secure and effective system.

In the context of Quorum Authorization (QAuth) management, understanding the roles and responsibilities of different actors is important for maintaining a secure and effective system. The key actors include RSC administrator, QAuth administrator, QAuth approver, QAuth viewer, and RSC user.

Similar to the Global organization, these QAuth actors can be designated at the tenant organization level. This allows tenants to independently manage their specific QAuth workflows.
Note:
While tenants have the autonomy to manage these configurations, the Global organization retains oversight and centralized control across all tenant-level QAuth configurations and policies.

RSC administrator

A RSC administrator is any user with one of the static roles of Administrator or Owner, or any user who has been assigned the Manage user access privilege through a custom role. In a multitenant environment, this includes administrators within a tenant organization.
Note:
Users with static roles, such as Administrator or Owner, cannot be approvers. Similarly, users with the Manage user access privilege in custom roles cannot be approvers.
The key responsibilities of the RSC administrator include:
  • Enabling the QAuth feature: Handling the initial setup of the QAuth feature, which includes enabling it on RSC.
  • Selecting the first QAuth administrator: Choosing the first QAuth administrator, who must be a local user.
  • Managing QAuth administrators: Adding or removing QAuth administrators.
  • Managing QAuth policies: Creating, editing, and deleting QAuth policies, and mapping these policies to specific roles for viewing or approval.
  • Assigning roles: Assigning QAuth roles to users based on system needs.
  • Viewing policies: Comprehensive visibility over all QAuth policies.
  • Managing QAuth settings: Editing QAuth settings, such as time-to-live (TTL) for requests and the quorum required for administrative actions.
  • Disabling the QAuth feature: When necessary, the RSC administrator can disable the QAuth feature, but this requires approval from a QAuth administrator.
Several RSC administrator actions require approval from a QAuth administrator:
  • Disabling the QAuth feature.
  • Adding additional QAuth administrators.
  • Editing and deleting QAuth policies.
  • Modifying QAuth settings for TTL and QAuth requirements.

Example scenario: If a RSC administrator wants to disable the QAuth feature due to a critical update requirement, a QAuth administrator must approve this action to ensure consensus and consideration of security requirements.

QAuth administrator

A QAuth administrator is any user with the static QAuth administrator role assigned in RSC. Users with this role can also be designated as QAuth approvers for QAuth policies.
Note:
Users in the QAuth administrator role cannot simultaneously be RSC administrators or hold the Manage user access privilege received from an assigned custom role. If conflicts exist, RSC removes the QAuth administrator role until the conflict is resolved. The first QAuth administrator must be a local user.
The key responsibilities of the QAuth administrator include:
  • Approval of configuration changes: Approves system-level configuration changes.
  • Management of QAuth administrators: Approves the addition and deletion of more QAuth administrators.
  • Policy modifications: Modifies QAuth policies and approves users for roles involving the QAuth administrator.
Certain QAuth administrator actions require peer approval:
  • Approving users for being assigned the QAuth administrator role.
  • Approving the disabling of the QAuth feature.

Example scenario: If a QAuth administrator proposes adding a new QAuth administrator, another QAuth administrator must approve this action to maintain a system of checks and balances.

QAuth approver

The QAuth approver role enables users who are not RSC administrators to approve and deny requests. These QAuth approvers can have full permissions for managing system configuration or data management. Their actions can also trigger QAuth approvals.

The QAuth approver role is not fixed. It can change based on the permissions assigned within custom roles by granting the Approve and deny requests privilege. Optional privileges include View Requests or View Policies. QAuth approvers review change requests related to assigned policies and decide whether to approve or deny them.
Note:
A QAuth approver may be a local user or part of an SSO group. However, the first QAuth administrator must be a local user.
Some limitations of the QAuth approver role include:
  • Users with the Manage user access permission cannot be QAuth approvers.
  • Users cannot approve their own action requests to avoid conflicts of interest.
Best practices for designating the QAuth approver include:
  • Redundancy and availability: Assigning at least two approvers for each QAuth policy to ensure availability.
  • Emergency approvers: Maintaining a small group of management-level emergency approvers who can approve any policy-based requests.
  • Contextual privilege: Granting the View privilege for the object or SLA Domain associated with the action to be approved. This allows approvers to understand the details involved. Without this privilege, approvers rely solely on the basic information provided in the request details.
  • Dedicated roles: Creating dedicated custom roles for approvers named after the policy or workload they oversee.
Example scenario: If a department needs to quickly process a policy change, having multiple approvers and emergency approvers ensures that the process does not stall due to the unavailability of a single person.

QAuth viewer

Any user can hold the QAuth viewer role. This user may also hold other RSC permissions for managing system configuration or data management. Their actions may trigger a QAuth approval. QAuth viewers can review changes or policies but cannot take any request actions.

The QAuth viewer role includes the View Requests and View Policies privileges, which custom roles grant. A QAuth viewer can be a local user or part of an SSO group.

Example scenario: A QAuth viewer can observe a pending policy change to report any inconsistencies to the management, ensuring transparency and oversight.

RSC user

A RSC user is defined as any user who does not hold the specific QAuth roles of RSC administrator, QAuth administrator, QAuth approver, or QAuth viewer. The RSC users perform their jobs based on assigned roles, potentially triggering QAuth approvals based on their actions.

Example scenario: A RSC user attempting to access sensitive data may trigger a QAuth policy, thus requiring a QAuth approver to review and authorize this access to maintain data security.
